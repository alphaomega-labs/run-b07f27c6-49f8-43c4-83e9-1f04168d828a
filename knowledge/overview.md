# Literature Overview: Quantum Reservoirs, Kernels, Entanglement, and Baseline Parity

This distillation synthesizes the 38-source corpus indexed in knowledge/source_index.json for the user question in user_input: whether quantum reservoirs with PCA-based angle encoding can outperform matched classical reservoirs, and how entanglement changes feature mapping under tight compute. The corpus spans foundational reservoir-computing theory (url:echo_state_2001, doi:10.1162/089976602760407955), quantum-kernel formalism (arxiv:2101.11020, arxiv:2008.08605), entanglement-focused classification studies (arxiv:2209.05142, arxiv:2509.06873), and a dense 2024-2026 wave of QRC/QELM architecture, feedback, measurement, and hardware papers (arxiv:2403.08998, arxiv:2407.02553, arxiv:2409.00998, arxiv:2412.06758, arxiv:2503.17939, arxiv:2503.22380, arxiv:2602.00610, arxiv:2602.14677, arxiv:2602.17440, arxiv:2602.18377, arxiv:2602.19700, arxiv:2602.21544, arxiv:2603.21371, and companions).

Across this corpus, one high-confidence technical anchor is the memory-nonlinearity framing in arxiv:2603.21371. Unlike many records that currently preserve equation snippets but sparse assumption/claim scaffolding, this source provides explicit assumptions and claims plus evidence atoms. The key decomposition IPC_tot = IPC_1 + IPC_{>=2} separates linear memory from nonlinear transformation capacity and is evaluated against Lorenz-63 and Mackey-Glass dynamics. This gives a reusable lens for the planned entangling-vs-non-entangling ablation: entanglement is not a universal on/off advantage, but a control that shifts the operating point along memory vs nonlinearity trade-offs.

The second stable anchor is the kernel interpretation of supervised quantum models (arxiv:2101.11020) and encoding expressivity analysis (arxiv:2008.08605). The kernel view formalizes quantum feature maps through density-state overlaps such as kappa(x,x' ) = tr[rho(x) rho(x' )], making QRC/QELM readout comparison legible against classical kernel baselines. The encoding paper argues expressive power is strongly determined by data-injection choice, not only readout or trainable parameters. Together, these imply a fair quantum-advantage claim requires parity in data preprocessing, readout family/regularization budget, and effective feature capacity.

This parity requirement aligns with classical reservoir foundations in url:echo_state_2001 and doi:10.1162/089976602760407955, where fixed recurrent dynamics plus a trained linear readout can already solve temporal tasks. If PCA components already linearize class structure, a classical random-feature or echo-state baseline can absorb much of apparent gain unless quantum models are tested in regimes where nonlinear memory and measurement geometry remain decisive. The user's concern that MNIST may saturate is therefore source-consistent, not merely intuition.

Within the 2024-2026 comparator lineage, three methodological fronts recur. First, measurement/operator design is repeatedly treated as the dominant bottleneck and optimization lever. arxiv:2602.14677 explicitly formulates measurement-operator optimization for stateless and stateful QRC variants, reframing performance questions from quantum vs classical substrate to which observables reveal task-relevant nonlinear statistics under shot constraints. Second, architecture and substrate diversification is substantial: spin networks (arxiv:2403.08998), analog Rydberg platforms (arxiv:2407.02553, arxiv:2602.00610), superconducting repeated-measurement protocols (arxiv:2310.06706), optical interferometric feedback (arxiv:2602.17440), and Wiener-style hybrids (arxiv:2601.04812). Third, tasks move beyond toy classification toward forecasting, denoising, molecular property prediction, radar/SAR, and finance (arxiv:2602.21544, arxiv:2602.13094, arxiv:2512.18612, arxiv:2412.06758, arxiv:2601.22194, arxiv:2512.11367).

Consensus is strongest on operational statements. A first consensus point is that fixed quantum reservoirs are highly sensitive to measured observables; architecture richness does not automatically convert into readout gains (arxiv:2602.14677, arxiv:2503.17939, arxiv:2503.22380, arxiv:2602.17440). A second consensus point is that entanglement effects are conditional. Works explicitly discussing entanglement advantages (arxiv:2403.08998, arxiv:2209.05142, arxiv:2509.06873) still coexist with kernel and encoding arguments showing that classical kernels or highly separable embeddings can explain much of observed performance (arxiv:2101.11020, arxiv:2008.08605). A third consensus point is that NISQ noise and shot budgets can erase representational gains (arxiv:2601.23084, arxiv:2601.22194, arxiv:2407.02553, arxiv:2602.00610).

Contradictions in this corpus are mostly regime contradictions rather than theorem-level logical negations. The first contradiction class concerns entanglement efficacy. Positive or conditional-advantage evidence is reported in arxiv:2403.08998 and arxiv:2209.05142, while arxiv:2509.06873 simultaneously highlights entanglement relevance and classical simulability boundaries. The contradiction is extendable if downstream phases sweep dissipation, depth, observable budget, and shot count; it is blocking only if the paper tries to claim binary universal superiority without regime qualification.

The second contradiction class concerns benchmark adequacy for advantage claims. Image-classification demonstrations exist (arxiv:2409.00998, arxiv:2212.08693), but corpus direction and user constraints suggest MNIST-like tasks can ceiling out after PCA. Applied papers reporting meaningful gains usually involve harder data geometry or dynamics (arxiv:2412.06758 molecular properties, arxiv:2601.22194 radar micro-Doppler, arxiv:2512.11367 SAR, arxiv:2602.13094 market data). This is an extendable contradiction resolved by a tiered benchmark suite rather than a single dataset.

The third contradiction class concerns origin of gains: are improvements due to intrinsic reservoir dynamics or to optimized measurement/readout/kernel geometry? Measurement-centric sources (arxiv:2602.14677, arxiv:2101.11020) support the second explanation, while hardware-centric architecture papers emphasize dynamics and many-body substrate effects (arxiv:2407.02553, arxiv:2602.00610, arxiv:2602.17440). Downstream experiment design must isolate these factors with factorial controls: fixed dynamics and varied observables, fixed observables and varied dynamics, and matched classical random-feature controls.

Notation and equation coverage is uneven across the indexed corpus. Most records include key-equation snippets, but explicit assumptions, claims, limitations, and future-work fields are populated for only three sources (arxiv:2603.21371, doi:10.1162/089976602760407955, url:echo_state_2001). This incompleteness does not invalidate synthesis, but it constrains confidence for fine-grained contradiction resolution. Therefore, notation seeds should prioritize symbols that are robust across source families: encoded input u_t or x, reservoir state rho_t or s_t, measured observables O_m, feature vector phi_t = [<O_1>_t,...,<O_M>_t], linear readout y_hat_t = W_out phi_t, kernel kappa(x_i,x_j)=tr[rho(x_i)rho(x_j)], and capacity decomposition IPC_1, IPC_{>=2}, IPC_tot. For the user-targeted setting, transverse Ising controls (J, h), dissipation/noise gamma, and shot budget N_shot should be first-class variables.

Novelty boundaries are clear enough for a defensible contribution. Established facts: reservoir-readout separation can work in both classical and quantum regimes; encoding and measurement choices materially affect outcomes; entanglement can help in specific regimes but is not automatically beneficial; and hardware noise/shot limits can negate gains. Contested facts: whether entangling reservoirs deliver robust net advantage under strict baseline parity on realistic image tasks; whether observed gains originate mainly in dynamics or mainly in measurement/readout optimization; and whether large analog platforms maintain gains after calibration/sampling overhead. Open contribution space: a rigorously controlled resource-bounded benchmark mapping the region where entanglement plus observable optimization improves accuracy/error trade-offs over matched classical reservoirs under identical preprocessing.

Equation-level comparison also reveals why direct score comparison across papers is hard. arxiv:2603.21371 explicitly couples benchmark dynamics (Lorenz-63 and Mackey-Glass) to an IPC decomposition, producing a capacity-oriented lens. By contrast, arxiv:2101.11020 formalizes model behavior via kernel overlaps and RKHS-style reasoning, while arxiv:2008.08605 focuses on encoding-induced expressivity. arxiv:2602.14677 introduces operator-optimization framing around a fixed quantum feature map, and arxiv:2601.23084 introduces noise-aware margin language under local depolarizing channels. These mathematical frames are individually coherent but not directly commensurate. A downstream paper that wants strong comparative claims should pre-register a bridge table converting each frame into shared observables: task loss, calibration, stability under shot reduction, and compute-normalized performance. Without that bridge, one can claim local superiority in each frame while still failing to establish robust cross-frame advantage.

Assumption-level comparison is similarly uneven. Classical reservoir sources assume stable input-driven transients and linear decodability, with performance largely controlled by dynamical richness and readout fit. Quantum-kernel sources assume that the feature map geometry governs separability and generalization behavior. Entanglement-focused sources assume that multi-qubit correlations can enrich that geometry, but they also acknowledge dependence on circuit family, noise channel, and training protocol. Hardware-oriented QRC sources implicitly assume that control and calibration overheads remain bounded relative to gains. In the present indexed corpus, assumption fields are explicit in only a small subset, so this synthesis treats several assumptions as inferred from summaries/titles and marks those in contradiction and open-problem entries as requiring closure during literature_review and derive_math_methodology. This explicit inferential marking is important: it prevents accidental overclaiming from sparse extraction metadata.

Failure-region mapping should be first-class in downstream validation. Three failure regions recur in this corpus and should be preserved as non-negotiable checks. Region A is separability saturation: once PCA rank is high enough on easy datasets, both classical and quantum reservoirs can approach ceiling performance, obscuring genuine representational differences. Region B is measurement starvation: too few observables or poorly chosen operators collapse effective feature diversity, making entanglement toggles appear irrelevant. Region C is hardware-noise and sampling stress: under depolarization, finite shots, and calibration drift, nominal quantum advantages can invert. Therefore, each candidate problem statement in this phase pairs a plausible contribution with a corresponding failure test artifact. Success should mean not only better mean accuracy but also bounded degradation across these failure regions under matched budget.
